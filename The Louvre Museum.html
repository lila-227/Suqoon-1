The Louvre Museum

The choice to talk about the Louvre Museum was accidental, but to be honest, it was one of the museums that amazed me when I was young. When I watched the Ladybug cartoon, most of the scenes were in this museum, which increased my curiosity and love for it. But over time, I learned about more museums.

The Louvre Museum is a national art museum in Paris, France, and one of the most famous museums in the world.

Located on the right bank of the Seine River in Paris, the capital of France, the Louvre is the world's largest art gallery, showcasing works from numerous human civilizations. Among its treasures is the famous Mona Lisa by Leonardo da Vinci. Originally, the museum was a fortress built by Philip Augustus in 1190 to protect the city from surprise attacks during his frequent absences on Crusades. The fortress took its name from the location on which it was built. Later, it became a royal palace known as the Louvre Palace, inhabited by the kings of France. The last monarch to use it as his official residence was Louis XIV, who moved to the Palace of Versailles in 1672, making it the new seat of government. The Louvre then became a repository for a collection of royal treasures, particularly sculptures.

In 1692, the building housed two academies, one for painting and sculpture, which held their first salons in 1699. They occupied the building for 100 years. During the French Revolution, the National Assembly declared that the Louvre should become a national museum to display the nation's masterpieces. The museum opened on August 10, 1793. It is divided into several sections according to art type and period. Its galleries cover approximately 13 square kilometers and contain more than one million works of art, including paintings and sculptures.

The museum boasts a magnificent collection of Greek, Roman, Egyptian, and Mesopotamian antiquities—numbering 5,664 pieces—as well as paintings and sculptures dating back to the 18th century.
