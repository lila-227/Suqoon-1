The Scream

The Scream is the common name given to one of the most famous works of art by the Norwegian Expressionist artist Edvard Munch, painted in 1893. The original German title given to it by Munch was The Scream of Nature (Der Schrei der Natur) and the Norwegian title is Scream (Skrik). The anguished face in the painting has become one of the most famous images of art, seen as symbolizing concern about the human condition.

In the foreground of the painting, we see a railway track, and across the track, a figure with their hands raised to their head, their eyes wide with terror and their mouth screaming. In the background, two figures wearing hats are visible, and behind them, a landscape of hills.

Munch painted the picture after a psychological experience he went through and wrote about in his diary: “I was walking along the road with two friends when the sun set, and I felt a touch of melancholy. Suddenly the sky turned blood red, so I stopped and leaned against a hedge by the side of the road, overcome by indescribable exhaustion. Then I looked at the flaming clouds hanging like blood and a sword over the cliff of the blue sea that tended toward black in the city. My friends continued walking, but I stopped there, trembling with fear. Then I heard a scream echoing endlessly in nature.”

In his book, The Age of the Image, published by the World of Knowledge series, January 2005, Dr. Shaker Abdel Hamid says, “Edvar Munch’s famous painting, The Scream, for example, which he painted in 1893, was intended to depict that particular pain of modern life, and it has become an icon signifying neurosis and human fear. In the original painting, the red sky creates an overall feeling of anxiety and fear, and the central figure in it is like a ghostly embodiment of anxiety…”
