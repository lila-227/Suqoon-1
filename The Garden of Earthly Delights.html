The Garden of Earthly Delights

The Garden of Earthly Delights, or Paradise of Earthly Pleasures, is the most famous work of art by the Dutch artist Hieronymus Bos.

It is an allegorical oil painting measuring 220 by 389, painted between 1500 and 1505, shortly before his death. This artwork, imbued with a moral message, is considered one of his most mysterious, complex, and beautiful works.

The painting is divided into three sections, each depicting a different scene. The left section shows Paradise, including the creation of Eve and the fountain of life, while the right section shows Hell. The middle section, which gives the painting its title, represents the pleasures and delights of life. Between Paradise and Hell, these pleasures are merely temptations to commit sins and transgressions, and their diversity reflects the varied inclinations of humankind towards worldly pleasures.

The trilogy concludes with a crystal depicting the third day of creation, with God the Father (according to Christian belief) as the creator. Two inscriptions on the doors read: "The Lord himself spoke the word, and all things were accomplished," and "The Lord himself commanded it, and all things were created."
