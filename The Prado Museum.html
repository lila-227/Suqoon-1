The Prado Museum

This is the museum that displays the painting "Saturn Devouring His Son."

The Prado Museum in Madrid, the capital of Spain, is an art museum and gallery, and one of the most important museums in Europe. It houses numerous artistic treasures and one of the world's finest collections of European art, spanning from the 12th to the early 19th centuries, based on the collection of the former Spanish monarchy.

The Prado Museum is a resting place for the giants of Spanish art.

It is one of the buildings erected during the reign of Charles III as part of a grand urban development plan for Madrid.
Originally intended to house the collections of the Institute of Natural Sciences, "Prado" (Spanish for "meadow") gave its name to the Prado Salon, later the Prado Promenade, and eventually to the museum itself.
Construction of the Prado Museum was interrupted between the reign of Charles III and during the Peninsular War, and resumed under Charles III's grandson, Ferdinand VII.
It was inaugurated on November 19, 1819, during the reign of Ferdinand VII. The Prado building was used as a cavalry barracks and gunpowder depot for Napoleon's forces based in Madrid during the War of Independence.
In fact, a visit to the Prado Museum opens a wide door to understanding the origins and achievements of Western art as a whole.
